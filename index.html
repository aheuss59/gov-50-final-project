Introduction

Coming to Cambridge this fall, I had lots of things on my mind. Class choices, new roommates, new friends, and (perhaps more importantly) coffee. As I learned soon after arriving, coffee is not hard to find in Cambridge. This project is an effort to explore where coffee locations are located in the United States and why. After exploring my data with more depth, I realized that it would be ineffective and misleading to attempt to analyze all coffee shops based on the provided data. As a result, this project focuses on the chain coffee franchises Dunkin’ Donuts and Starbucks. My primary research question was inspired by the sheer prevalence of coffee in Cambridge and asks: is there a correlation between these chain coffee locations and whether or not the city it is located in is a college town?

The Data

My original data was taken from Friendly Cities on GitHub and includes 800,000 restaurants throughout the United States. The dataset included the variables: RestaurantName, Cuisine, OpenHours, State, CNTY_GEOID, UA_NAME, MSA_NAME, MSA_GEOID, Lon, Lat, Frequency, and isChain. This dataset was originally used to find correlation between chain restaurants and a number of independent variables, so this project goes in another direction. The primary variables I focused on included RestaurantName, which provided the name of the restaurant, UA_NAME, which is short for Urban Area name and was typically formatted as the city and state where the restaurant was located, lastly the State variable, which provided the state abbreviation for the state the restaurant was located in. As the dataset originally stood, I did not have enough data to explore my research question. I created a new tibble labeled college_towns which includes the name of every town in the United States that has a four-year higher education institution located within it. This tibble was compiled using information provided on Campus Explorer and then used to create the variable college_town within the dataset. The final two variables I added to the dataset were based on location and are called region and division. These variables were created in reference to the regions and divisions assigned to states by the United States Census Bureau.

The dependent variable in my study is whether or not a town has a chain coffee location, in this study refering to a Dunkin’ Donuts or a Starbucks.

Analysis 1

Of the sampled Dunkin’ Donuts and Starbucks locations, there appears to be a correlation between location of restaurants and college towns. The following table calculates the percentage of sampled Dunkin’ Donuts and Starbucks locations that are located in college towns as opposed to non-college towns. Additionally, the average treatment effect of the town being a college town as opposed to a non-college town is calculated as the Average Treatment Effect.

props <- coffee_data |>
  mutate(new_name = if_else(RestaurantName == "Dunkin", "Dunkin' Donuts", "Starbucks")) |>
  group_by(new_name, college_town) |>
  summarize(count = n()) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = college_town, values_from = count) |>
  mutate(perc_college_town = (`1`/(`1` + `0`)) * 100,
         perc_non_college_town = (`0`/(`1` + `0`)) * 100,
         ate = perc_college_town - perc_non_college_town) 

props |>
  select(perc_college_town, perc_non_college_town, ate) |>
  knitr :: kable(caption = "Percent of Sampled Chain Coffee Locations in College versus Non-College Towns", col.names = c("Restaurant Name", "College Town (%)", "Non-College Town (%)", "Average Treatment Effect (%)"), digits = 2)

Restaurant Name College Town (%) Non-College Town (%) Average Treatment Effect (%)
Dunkin’ Donuts 80.54 19.46 61.07
Starbucks 82.41 17.59 64.81

These results suggest that there is a fairly substansive difference between the percent of Dunkin’ Donuts and Starbucks locations that are located in college towns as opposed to non-college towns. There are approximately 61% more Dunkin’ Donuts locations in college towns and that number grows to nearly 65% for Starbucks.
